# The Bones of Saltrest

*A D&D 5e adventure hook for a party of level 5.*

## The Hook

You arrive at Saltrest on a gray morning when the fishing boats are being hosed down with seawater and salt. Something is wrong here.

The nets that went out three weeks ago have come up empty or lined with something worse—human bones, small and bleached, tangled in the cordage like a macabre catch. The fishers won't talk about it much beyond short, angry words. They just want the nets burned. At the Salted Anchor, the tavern's common room is half-full of people nursing drinks in daylight, and the mood is one of coiled dread. Someone is missing: **Mayor Halrik**, the village leader, rowed out alone last Thursday toward the **sea caves on the north shore**—the ones the locals call **Kelpwater Cove**—and never returned. His widow waits at his house. The village's guards have not gone looking.

**Mother Vell**, the priest who tends to the shrine of the drowned saint, has been seen at her temple since it happened, and when asked if she will help, she refuses flatly: no prayer will take her near the water. Her hands shake when she speaks of it.

The day before the mayor vanished, a **necromancer named Coria Sleet** was spotted at the market buying rope, lanterns, and wax—supplies for something underground and lengthy. The merchant remembers her because she was kind, almost gentle, asking after the caves as if planning a scholarly expedition. She left town after that. No one has seen her since.

The party is asked directly: find Mayor Halrik. Stop whatever is killing the fishery. The pay is modest—what the village can afford—but the stakes are clear. If the bones keep coming and the water stays dead, Saltrest will starve by winter.

Three threads pull the party in different directions: the caves themselves, waiting in the cliffs; the mystery of the necromancer's purpose and present whereabouts; and Mother Vell's terror, which hints at something unholy already known.

## Cast & Factions

## Mayor Halrik (Missing)

You know him as the aging anchor of Saltrest—weathered hands, salt-grey beard, the voice that settles disputes at the Salted Anchor. Three weeks ago, the nets began coming up empty or threaded with bone. Last Thursday night, you watched him row out alone toward the north caves, his boat's lantern cutting the dark water. He hasn't returned.

**What actually happened:** Halrik rowed to the caves seeking the cause of the strange catch. There he encountered Coria Sleet at work—not as a threat, but as something far stranger. He let her bind him with rope and lead him deeper into the Vault, where he now kneels beside the Seraphine ledger with the other Drowned Choir initiates, his hands pale and wet, pressing the ship's manifest into the black water. He is not dead, but he is no longer himself. He still knows Saltrest; he still remembers his daughter. This makes him dangerous to reach and impossible to save without destroying what he has become.

**What the party may discover:** Witnesses saw a second figure with Halrik that night—cloaked, moving with unnatural grace. A rope-seller remembers Halrik bought lanterns weeks before, not days. The drowned themselves know the mayor by name and feel no malice toward him. They speak of him with something like gratitude.

---

## Mother Vell (The Cleric)

The priest of a drowned-saint faith—a goddess of sailors lost at sea, whose mercy comes in the form of a swift death and a song sung over still water. Mother Vell is iron-thin, eyes storm-grey, and her fingers are stained with candlewax. She stands at the temple door each morning and refuses to bless the fishing boats anymore.

**What she fears:** Three weeks ago, Mother Vell performed the funeral rites for a drowned sailor pulled from the nets—a man named Kellan, whom she'd known since childhood. The next night, Kellan walked up the beach with seaweed in his hair and salt pooling in his mouth, his eyes open and empty. He called her name once, then turned back to the water. Since then, she has refused to bless the water, refused to touch fish still wet from the nets, and flinches from the sound of rowing.

**What she knows:** Something is wrong with death itself. The dead are not resting. She suspects Coria Sleet and magic, but more than that—she suspects her own god has abandoned Saltrest, or worse, that something older than her faith is calling the dead home. She has not left the temple in two weeks. She will not cross the breakwater.

**Why the party should listen:** Mother Vell cannot fight, but she knows the old drowned-saint rites—prayers that ease restless spirits, rituals to lay the dead to rest properly. She is a potential ally, if the party can convince her that her god still has work for her to do. Her faith is a resource; her refusal to act is a consequence.

---

## Coria Sleet (The Necromancer)

She is not what the village whispers. Coria is tall and precise, her robes waterlogged and clinging, her lips faintly blue with cold. She speaks in a low, certain voice—never raised, never rushed. When she smiles, it is the smile of someone doing difficult work that no one else will do.

**Her true motive:** Coria Sleet is reading the Seraphine ledger aloud to the Drowned Choir. The ship that carried them—the Seraphine—went down seventeen years ago with 143 souls. Their names were never spoken again. No prayer was sung. No one called them home.

Coria believes that raising the drowned and speaking their names aloud will *carry the souls home*—a mercy, not a curse. She sees herself as a conductor of a necessary rite, a priest of the forgotten dead. She does not hate Saltrest; she barely notices it. The village is incidental. What matters is that 143 souls, written in a waterlogged ledger in the Vault, will finally be named in the world of the living.

**What she is doing now:** Coria has bound the strongest of the drowned—including Mayor Halrik—to the Seraphine ledger in the Vault's flooded chamber. Each round they work without interruption, the ledger sinks deeper into the black water. In five rounds, it will be fully submerged (recovery requires a `DC 15 Strength (Athletics)` check). In ten rounds, the pages will dissolve entirely. Once the ledger is gone, the names can never be read aloud, and Coria's mercy becomes an eternal entombment.

**What the party may discover:** Coria was not always a necromancer. She was a sailor's widow—the Seraphine was her wife's ship. She watched the list of the dead posted on the dock, then never mentioned again. She learned magic to reach them. Every corpse she raised, every name she gathered, was an act of grief transformed into purpose. She will not surrender the ledger willingly. But she can be negotiated with, if someone understands what she is actually trying to do.

**What she knows:** The town thinks her a villain. She is indifferent to this. She knows that disruption in Saltrest will draw attention—adventurers, soldiers, priests. She is racing against time, not out of fear, but because the ledger is dissolving whether she works or not. The sea is taking it back. She is not destroying a cure; she is preserving a funeral rite.

---

## The Drowned Choir (The Antagonist Faction)

They are the crew of the Seraphine, seventeen years drowned, now raised by Coria and bound to a common purpose. Their bodies are waterlogged and pale, their movements slow and deliberate. Seawater drips from their clothes and beards. Some still wear the tatters of sailor's clothes. Some are missing fingers, pieces of faces. Where their eyes should show the white, there is only grey-green water.

**What they are:** They are not mindless undead. They remember their names, their shipmates, their drowning. They remember the dark water closing over them and the silence after. They speak in voices like distant waves, overlapping and sometimes harmonious. They do not hunger for flesh. They do not kill for malice.

**What they want:** They want their names spoken in the world of the living. They want to be remembered. They want the Seraphine ledger—the ship's manifest that lists them as *souls carried*—to be read aloud from beginning to end. Once this is done, Coria has promised, they will return to the deep and rest.

**How they move:** Coria has bound a core group of initiates to the Seraphine ledger itself—these are the strongest of the drowned, including Mayor Halrik. They kneel in the Vault with the ledger, pressing it deeper into the black water with a sinking curse. The rest of the Choir inhabit the three levels of Kelpwater Cove, guarding against interruption. They do not attack unless attacked; they guard. Some may warn intruders. Some may step aside, if approached with respect.

**Why they are not simply villains:** The Drowned Choir are the stakes of this adventure. The party can fight them and destroy them. They can negotiate with them and convince them to stand down. They can race to read the ledger and grant them peace. But they cannot ignore them, and they cannot save everyone. The souls of the Drowned Choir are the real terrain of this adventure—more real than any dungeon room.

**Concrete detail—the haunting:** On quiet nights in Saltrest, you can hear them singing. It sounds like the wind in the caves, like the sea working its way into wooden hulls, like voices underwater calling the names of people long dead. The song has no words, but it has a rhythm—a dirge, a roll call. Each night, it grows a little louder.

## Kelpwater Cove

The sea caves known as Kelpwater Cove claw into the north shore, a labyrinth of stone chambers carved by centuries of tide. The air tastes of brine and rot. The water runs deep and cold. Here, in the lowest chamber called the Vault, the Seraphine ledger sinks toward the dark, and with it the last peace the drowned sailors will know.

### Tide Cut

This is the entry level—a tidal chamber where waves still reach, where daylight filters through salt-spray and the cavern mouth gapes like a throat. The smell of kelp and dead fish is thick.

**The Chamber**: A twenty-foot-wide grotto. The tide line marks the stone two feet up—above it, pale barnacles; below, black slime. A stone ledge, slick with spray, runs along the eastern wall and offers safe passage. The western side is deep water, dark and unpleasant. Small rocks jut from the shallows. The cave mouth opens north, revealing grey ocean. Two passages lead deeper: a narrow stair down the southeast wall (to the Choir Gallery), and a flooded tunnel that sinks beneath the waterline to the west.

**What Lurks Here**: Three **Drowned Sailors** (risen by Coria's necromancy) patrol the chamber. They have corpse-pale skin, hollow eyes, and move with the jerking grace of things pulled up from the abyss.

**Drowned Sailor** — AC 11, 11 HP each
- Melee: trident, +3 to hit, 1d6+1 piercing
- Reaction: when a creature approaches the stone ledge, one sailor can move up to 15 feet toward it

**Hazards**: 
- The stone ledge is slick. A creature moving faster than half speed along it must succeed on a **DC 12 Dexterity (Acrobatics)** check or slide 5 feet and fall prone.
- The water is 12 feet deep where the ledge ends. Creatures who fall in take 1d4 bludgeoning damage and begin drowning if they cannot swim.

**Pressure**: The Drowned Sailors do not pursue beyond the ledge—they are bound to guard the chamber—but they are relentless. If killed, they reform in 1 hour, crawling up from the deep. This is not a place where a long rest is safe.

### The Choir Gallery

Descend the southeast stair and the stone opens into a vast, vaulted chamber. Here the waves cannot reach. The walls are crusted with shell and bleached bone. Dozens of candles flicker in alcoves, casting shadows that move like choir members swaying.

**The Chamber**: A cathedral-like space, sixty feet long, forty feet wide, twenty feet tall. The floor slopes downward toward the north end, where it reaches a second tier—a raised platform of stone, dry and somehow colder than the rest of the cave. Stone benches line the walls as if meant for pews. Water channels cut across the floor, all flowing northward and downward. Scattered along the benches are small offerings: shells, coins tarnished green, sea-smoothed glass, human teeth.

To the north, a massive archway opens, and beyond it lies deeper dark—the passage to the Vault. Two alcoves flank the archway. The air here is very still.

**What Lurks Here**: Five **Drowned Initiates** (lesser servants of the Choir) stand or kneel along the benches, heads bowed. They are singing—a low, toneless chant in a language no living person speaks. They notice intruders but do not immediately act, waiting for a signal.

**Drowned Initiate** — AC 12, 8 HP each
- Melee: clawed hand, +2 to hit, 1d4 slashing
- Hymn (triggered if any initiate is attacked): Each initiate within 30 feet of the attacker can use its reaction to move 10 feet closer and make one clawed attack.

**Hazards**:
- The water channels are ankle-deep and icy cold. A creature moving through one must succeed on a **DC 13 Constitution (Endurance)** check or have disadvantage on attack rolls until the end of its next turn (cold shock).
- The stone benches are unstable. A creature that jumps or falls onto one must succeed on a **DC 11 Strength (Athletics)** check to land safely; failure means it tumbles backward, taking 1d6 bludgeoning damage and landing prone.

**Pressure**: The Initiates' toneless chanting grows louder as the party advances. The singing echoes until it seems to come from everywhere. A party that destroys the candles finds the Initiates fight harder in darkness—they gain advantage on attack rolls in dim light or darkness.

### The Vault

The archway opens into a circular chamber, perhaps eighty feet across, that descends into black water. The water is still as a mirror, and the light of raised torches barely reaches the far side. The air is thick and cold.

**The Chamber**: The Vault is a flooded pit. A stone platform, thirty feet across, rises from the center of the water like an island. A narrow stone bridge, slick and only three feet wide, connects it to the chamber's edge. The platform is carved with a spiral pattern worn smooth by centuries of water. At its exact center, two figures kneel beside a lectern holding an ancient, water-stained manuscript—the **Seraphine ledger**.

**The Seraphine Ledger**: A ship's manifest bound in salt-rotted leather, its pages yellowed and many illegible. The 143 names written inside are the souls of the drowned. Reading these names aloud is believed by Coria Sleet and the Choir to lay the dead to rest. If the ledger dissolves, this path closes.

**What Lurks Here**: Two **Drowned Choir Initiates** kneel pressing the ledger into the black water. They are larger than their younger siblings—more solid, more aware. They are Coria's lieutenants, and they sing in a harmony that makes the water ripple.

**Drowned Choir Initiate** — AC 12, 22 HP each
- Melee: trident, +4 to hit, 1d6+2 piercing
- Drowned Hymn (while within 10 feet of the ledger): advantage on Wisdom (Perception) checks; resistance to nonmagical bludgeoning, piercing, and slashing damage
- Reaction (on its turn): can use its reaction to press the ledger deeper

**The Ticking Clock**: This is the mechanical spine of the Vault. The two initiates are performing a sinking curse. Each round, the ledger sinks deeper into the black water. Track this explicitly:

- **Rounds 1–4**: The ledger is visible, resting on the platform edge. A creature can see its pages and the spiral binding. No action penalty.
- **Round 5**: The ledger is half-submerged. The pages are wet and brittle. To recover the ledger and pull it to safety, a creature must reach it (30 feet of movement across the bridge and water) and succeed on a **DC 15 Strength (Athletics)** check. Failure means the creature must immediately repeat the check or fall back into the water (which is 20 feet deep).
- **Rounds 6–9**: The ledger sinks further, pages coming apart. The check to recover it increases to **DC 17 Strength (Athletics)**. Many pages are already lost.
- **Round 10**: The ledger touches the lake bottom. The remaining pages dissolve into sediment. The "read the names aloud" resolution path closes. No check can recover it now.

**Hazards**:
- The black water is 20 feet deep and bitterly cold. A creature that falls in or enters it takes 1d6 cold damage immediately and must succeed on a **DC 14 Constitution (Endurance)** check or have disadvantage on attack rolls until the end of its next turn.
- The stone bridge is only three feet wide and slippery. A creature moving across it at more than half speed must succeed on a **DC 12 Dexterity (Acrobatics)** check or slide 5 feet in a direction of your choice and fall prone.
- **Drowning**: A creature that is unconscious in the water begins drowning. It can hold its breath for 1 minute before it must start making **DC 10 Constitution** checks, beginning with one failure. On the third failure, it dies.

**Multiple Paths to Engage**:

1. **Combat Approach**: Kill or disable the two initiates before round 10. They will fight to protect the ledger and will attempt to flee if bloodied (below 11 HP). If they flee, they move toward deeper water and attempt to trigger an alarm (a low sonorous note that brings reinforcements from the Choir Gallery in 1d4+1 rounds).

2. **Ledger Recovery Approach**: Bypass or ignore the initiates entirely and attempt to cross the bridge and recover the ledger before it dissolves. A party that succeeds on the **DC 15 Strength (Athletics)** check (or higher, depending on round) can retrieve the ledger and retreat with it—preserving the "read the names aloud" resolution. The initiates will pursue, but the primary objective is secured.

3. **Ritual Disruption Approach**: A party that understands the sinking curse (via investigation, divination, or NPC information) can attempt to interrupt the initiates' coordination. If a creature uses an action to make a **DC 16 Charisma (Deception or Insight)** check, it can convince one initiate to stop pressing the ledger for 1 round. This buys time to escape or fight.

4. **Environmental Manipulation Approach**: Clever parties might redirect water flow, collapse part of the bridge, or seal the platform to prevent further sinking. These require success on **DC 13 Intelligence (Arcana or Investigation)** checks and may take more than one round to set up, but they bypass the need to fight or recover the ledger directly.

**Pressure and Tone**: The moment the party enters the Vault, narrate the weight of the space—the cold, the singing of the initiates, the sound of water dripping. On each round, note the ledger's descent: "Round 4: the water climbs to the binding... Round 5: the first page touches the surface... Round 8: the pages are almost gone." This is not a puzzle to solve once; it is a living timer that forces decision-making under pressure.

If the party dawdles or splits focus, the initiates will not wait. They will press harder, and the sinking will accelerate. The ledger is not a passive prop—its deterioration is a clock, and clocks count down whether the party is ready or not.

## Resolution

The adventure's three distinct paths emerge from the Vault's ticking clock and the Drowned Choir's singular purpose. Each leads to a fundamentally different end state for Saltrest, its survivors, and the drowned souls seeking peace.

### Path 1 — Combat / Descend and Destroy

You and your party fight through Kelpwater Cove level by level, dispatching or bypassing the Drowned Sailors in Tide Cut, facing down the singing Initiates in the Choir Gallery, and reaching the Vault to confront the two Drowned Choir Initiates pressing the Seraphine ledger into the black water.

**Trigger:** Hostility with the Drowned Choir, or choosing to make your way toward the Vault with the intent to fight.

**What It Requires:** Defeating or disabling the two Drowned Choir Initiates in the Vault before round 10. These foes are formidable—AC 12, 22 HP each, with tridents (+4 to hit, 1d6+2 piercing) and resistance to nonmagical damage while near the ledger. The water around you is 20 feet deep and cold. The bridge is narrow and slick. If you kill them, they do not rise again.

**Outcome if Successful:** The ledger remains in your hands. Coria Sleet, seeing her work undone, does not flee—she stands in the Vault doorway, grief-stricken and furious, and fights to the last. Once she falls, the curse binding the Drowned Choir to their purpose breaks. The remaining drowned—those still guarding the levels above—collapse into stillness. Over the next few days, their bodies wash ashore on Saltrest's beaches, and the village performs a proper funeral rite. Mother Vell, watching the dead finally at rest, regains her faith and blesses the fishing boats once more. The nets, within a week, begin to fill with fish again.

**Reward:** Coria's spellbook (a waterlogged tome containing *raise dead*, *speak with dead*, and *dispel magic*, among other necromancy spells) and a signet ring she wore—a relic of her wife, the Seraphine's lost captain. Mayor Halrik, freed from the binding curse but still drowned and hollow-eyed, is brought back to his widow. He lives, but he is not the man he was. He remembers the Vault. He remembers the ledger sinking. This memory will haunt him for the rest of his life, and Saltrest will have to choose whether to accept him as their leader or turn him away. The village's fishing industry recovers fully within a season.

**Consequence:** The mercy path closes forever. The 143 names on the Seraphine ledger will never be read aloud. The dead do rest—but they rest forgotten, as they have for seventeen years. The ledger itself, once recovered, begins to decay rapidly in sunlight and air. Within a month, it is illegible. Within a year, it is dust. This is a victory, but it is not without its cost—the dead are denied the final witness Coria believed they deserved.

---

### Path 2 — Town Defense / Hold the Breakwater

You do not pursue the Drowned Choir into Kelpwater Cove. Instead, you learn from Mother Vell or your own investigation that the Choir is strong but not infinite—that Coria's primary goal is the ledger, not conquest of Saltrest. You choose to defend the village and wait for the drowned to come ashore at the breakwater.

**Trigger:** Choosing to fortify Saltrest, organize the fishing families and guards, and prepare a defensive line at the breakwater where the drowned are most likely to emerge.

**What It Requires:** A successful DC 13 Charisma (Persuasion) check to convince Mayor Halrik's widow and the village militia captain to trust your plan. You must also decide how to handle Mother Vell—if you bring her into the defense, even in a non-combat role (blessing the breakwater, praying for the village's safety), the morale of Saltrest's people increases, and you gain advantage on rolls to steady the line when the drowned arrive. The confrontation will happen at the breakwater on the fifth night of your wait.

**Outcome if Successful:** On that fifth night, seventy-three of the Drowned Choir emerge from the cove and begin walking toward Saltrest proper. They are not fast—they move with the jerking, deliberate gait of things pulled from deep water. They do not run. They do not scream. They sing. Your defensive line holds. The party, positioned on the breakwater's high stones, can rain ranged attacks from above. The drowned are slow to climb. Some of your defenders fall—a fisherman, one of the militia—but the Choir does not reach the village proper. After an hour of combat, the Drowned Choir's singers falter. The spell holding them to purpose begins to unravel. They stop advancing. They simply stand in the shallow water, singing, as if waiting for something. If the party investigates, they realize: the ledger in the Vault is sinking. The Choir senses its dissolution. Their binding frays. By morning, the few remaining Drowned who came ashore have walked back into the sea and do not return. Coria, sensing the end, does not emerge from Kelpwater Cove.

**Reward:** The village hails your party as saviors. The immediate threat passes. Saltrest is safe, and its fishing industry, though wounded, begins a slow recovery. Each party member is offered a permanent room at the Salted Anchor, a modest annual stipend from the village coffers, and the rank of "Defender of Saltrest" (which grants advantage on Charisma checks when introducing yourselves in coastal communities). Mayor Halrik's widow offers the signet ring Halrik wore—a family heirloom of Saltrest's founding—as a token of gratitude. Mother Vell, her faith restored by the village's survival, offers blessings and a small crystal shaped like a tear-drop, which grants advantage on one saving throw related to fear or despair (usable once, then loses its magic).

**Consequence:** The Seraphine ledger dissolves in the Vault in the silence and dark. Coria, alone with her failure, eventually vanishes into the deep caves. She is not seen again. The 143 drowned names are never spoken aloud. They are denied the final mercy Coria believed she was offering. The remaining Drowned Choir do not rise again, but they do not rest in peace—they are merely gone, their purpose unfinished. Saltrest survives, but so does the haunting: on quiet nights, some fishers swear they can still hear the Choir's singing, faint and fading, from the direction of Kelpwater Cove. The song will grow quieter each year, but it will not stop for a generation.

---

### Path 3 — Mercy / Read the Ledger Aloud

You race into Kelpwater Cove not to destroy the Choir, but to recover the Seraphine ledger before it dissolves and read the 143 names aloud. This is the most desperate path—it requires understanding Coria's true purpose, believing (at least partly) that her mercy is genuine, and beating the clock before the ledger is lost forever.

**Trigger:** Learning that the ledger can grant the dead peace, that Coria's motive is grief and not malice, and choosing to retrieve and read it before round 10 of the sinking curse.

**What It Requires:** First, you must reach the Vault. This means navigating or defeating the Drowned Sailors in Tide Cut (AC 11, 11 HP each; they do not pursue beyond their chamber and do not pursue relentlessly if you retreat) and moving past or negotiating with the Drowned Initiates in the Choir Gallery (AC 12, 8 HP each; they will stand aside if you approach them with respect and show no hostility—a successful DC 14 Charisma (Persuasion or Insight) check suffices). Once in the Vault, you must cross the bridge and reach the platform without falling into the 20-foot-deep black water. The bridge is slick (DC 12 Dexterity (Acrobatics) to cross at more than half speed without falling prone). Time is critical: the ledger is visible and recoverable for rounds 1–4 of the sinking curse. At round 5, the ledger is half-submerged, and recovery requires a **DC 15 Strength (Athletics)** check. At rounds 6–9, it sinks deeper, the check increases to **DC 17**, and many pages are already lost. At round 10, the ledger touches the bottom, the pages dissolve, and this path closes.

The two Drowned Choir Initiates in the Vault (AC 12, 22 HP each) will not stop you if you convince them you intend to read the names—another DC 14 Charisma (Persuasion or Insight) check. If they believe you, they will pause their sinking curse for 1 round, giving you precious time to recover the ledger. If you fail to convince them or fight them, they will resume sinking the ledger faster, and the clock accelerates (the sinking advances by 2 rounds if both initiates are actively hostile). Mayor Halrik, one of the binding cursed, will recognize you if you have spoken to his widow. A successful DC 13 Wisdom (Insight) check allows you to understand that some part of him is trapped in the curse and does not wish to be there. He will not help—he cannot—but he will not actively hinder you.

**Outcome if Successful:** You retrieve the Seraphine ledger before round 10. You escape Kelpwater Cove (this may require further negotiation with the Choir or a final evasion). You return to Saltrest with the manifest. Coria does not follow—she remains in the Vault, broken and grieving. That evening, as the sun sets, you gather the village—Mother Vell, the fishing families, the militia, anyone who will listen. You stand on the breakwater and read the 143 names aloud, slowly and carefully, from the waterlogged pages of the Seraphine ledger. With each name, something settles in the water. The air warms slightly. The singing that has haunted Saltrest's nights grows quieter, then ceases. By the time you reach the last name, the sea is utterly still. The Drowned Choir does not rise that night, nor any night after. They have been given their final witness. They are at rest.

**Reward:** The Seraphine ledger itself, now a historical relic of Saltrest, grants you **three blessings of the Drowned**—each usable once, each granting advantage on one Constitution (Endurance) check related to water, drowning, or cold. The village offers a statue carved in your honor, erected on the breakwater. Mother Vell, her faith strengthened and her god affirmed in her belief, offers a personal blessing and grants you a **Water-Saint's Amulet** (a minor magical item that grants advantage on saving throws against being frightened or charmed while within sight of the sea or a large body of water). The fishing families, grateful and awestruck, give you the best catch of each season's first haul for a year (a modest but genuine gesture of gratitude). Mayor Halrik, freed from his binding curse the moment the last name is read, collapses on the beach. He is alive but traumatized. He will carry the memory of the Vault and the drowned for the rest of his life, but he is himself again—and he remembers that you chose mercy.

**Consequence:** Coria Sleet, her purpose fulfilled but her grief unresolved, walks deeper into Kelpwater Cove and does not emerge. The Drowned Sailors and Initiates return to the waters and to stillness. Kelpwater Cove becomes, once more, simply a place—haunted by memory, but not by active malice. The sea begins to be kind to Saltrest's fishers again. The nets begin to fill. But the ledger, having absorbed the seawater for seventeen years, continues to decay. The names, once read aloud, are lost to sight forever. Future generations of Saltrest will know that 143 souls were carried home, but they will not know their names. This is the cost of mercy—the dead are at peace, but the living must carry the burden of memory for them. This path offers the most moving resolution, but it is also the most ephemeral. The village will build songs and stories around what the party did, but the proof—the ledger itself—will eventually turn to dust. The dead are saved, but they are saved in silence.
