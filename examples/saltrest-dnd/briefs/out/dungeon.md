## Kelpwater Cove

The sea caves known as Kelpwater Cove claw into the north shore, a labyrinth of stone chambers carved by centuries of tide. The air tastes of brine and rot. The water runs deep and cold. Here, in the lowest chamber called the Vault, the Seraphine ledger sinks toward the dark, and with it the last peace the drowned sailors will know.

### Tide Cut

This is the entry level—a tidal chamber where waves still reach, where daylight filters through salt-spray and the cavern mouth gapes like a throat. The smell of kelp and dead fish is thick.

**The Chamber**: A twenty-foot-wide grotto. The tide line marks the stone two feet up—above it, pale barnacles; below, black slime. A stone ledge, slick with spray, runs along the eastern wall and offers safe passage. The western side is deep water, dark and unpleasant. Small rocks jut from the shallows. The cave mouth opens north, revealing grey ocean. Two passages lead deeper: a narrow stair down the southeast wall (to the Choir Gallery), and a flooded tunnel that sinks beneath the waterline to the west.

**What Lurks Here**: Three **Drowned Sailors** (risen by Coria's necromancy) patrol the chamber. They have corpse-pale skin, hollow eyes, and move with the jerking grace of things pulled up from the abyss.

**Drowned Sailor** — AC 11, 11 HP each
- Melee: trident, +3 to hit, 1d6+1 piercing
- Reaction: when a creature approaches the stone ledge, one sailor can move up to 15 feet toward it

**Hazards**: 
- The stone ledge is slick. A creature moving faster than half speed along it must succeed on a **DC 12 Dexterity (Acrobatics)** check or slide 5 feet and fall prone.
- The water is 12 feet deep where the ledge ends. Creatures who fall in take 1d4 bludgeoning damage and begin drowning if they cannot swim.

**Pressure**: The Drowned Sailors do not pursue beyond the ledge—they are bound to guard the chamber—but they are relentless. If killed, they reform in 1 hour, crawling up from the deep. This is not a place where a long rest is safe.

### The Choir Gallery

Descend the southeast stair and the stone opens into a vast, vaulted chamber. Here the waves cannot reach. The walls are crusted with shell and bleached bone. Dozens of candles flicker in alcoves, casting shadows that move like choir members swaying.

**The Chamber**: A cathedral-like space, sixty feet long, forty feet wide, twenty feet tall. The floor slopes downward toward the north end, where it reaches a second tier—a raised platform of stone, dry and somehow colder than the rest of the cave. Stone benches line the walls as if meant for pews. Water channels cut across the floor, all flowing northward and downward. Scattered along the benches are small offerings: shells, coins tarnished green, sea-smoothed glass, human teeth.

To the north, a massive archway opens, and beyond it lies deeper dark—the passage to the Vault. Two alcoves flank the archway. The air here is very still.

**What Lurks Here**: Five **Drowned Initiates** (lesser servants of the Choir) stand or kneel along the benches, heads bowed. They are singing—a low, toneless chant in a language no living person speaks. They notice intruders but do not immediately act, waiting for a signal.

**Drowned Initiate** — AC 12, 8 HP each
- Melee: clawed hand, +2 to hit, 1d4 slashing
- Hymn (triggered if any initiate is attacked): Each initiate within 30 feet of the attacker can use its reaction to move 10 feet closer and make one clawed attack.

**Hazards**:
- The water channels are ankle-deep and icy cold. A creature moving through one must succeed on a **DC 13 Constitution (Endurance)** check or have disadvantage on attack rolls until the end of its next turn (cold shock).
- The stone benches are unstable. A creature that jumps or falls onto one must succeed on a **DC 11 Strength (Athletics)** check to land safely; failure means it tumbles backward, taking 1d6 bludgeoning damage and landing prone.

**Pressure**: The Initiates' toneless chanting grows louder as the party advances. The singing echoes until it seems to come from everywhere. A party that destroys the candles finds the Initiates fight harder in darkness—they gain advantage on attack rolls in dim light or darkness.

### The Vault

The archway opens into a circular chamber, perhaps eighty feet across, that descends into black water. The water is still as a mirror, and the light of raised torches barely reaches the far side. The air is thick and cold.

**The Chamber**: The Vault is a flooded pit. A stone platform, thirty feet across, rises from the center of the water like an island. A narrow stone bridge, slick and only three feet wide, connects it to the chamber's edge. The platform is carved with a spiral pattern worn smooth by centuries of water. At its exact center, two figures kneel beside a lectern holding an ancient, water-stained manuscript—the **Seraphine ledger**.

**The Seraphine Ledger**: A ship's manifest bound in salt-rotted leather, its pages yellowed and many illegible. The 143 names written inside are the souls of the drowned. Reading these names aloud is believed by Coria Sleet and the Choir to lay the dead to rest. If the ledger dissolves, this path closes.

**What Lurks Here**: Two **Drowned Choir Initiates** kneel pressing the ledger into the black water. They are larger than their younger siblings—more solid, more aware. They are Coria's lieutenants, and they sing in a harmony that makes the water ripple.

**Drowned Choir Initiate** — AC 12, 22 HP each
- Melee: trident, +4 to hit, 1d6+2 piercing
- Drowned Hymn (while within 10 feet of the ledger): advantage on Wisdom (Perception) checks; resistance to nonmagical bludgeoning, piercing, and slashing damage
- Reaction (on its turn): can use its reaction to press the ledger deeper

**The Ticking Clock**: This is the mechanical spine of the Vault. The two initiates are performing a sinking curse. Each round, the ledger sinks deeper into the black water. Track this explicitly:

- **Rounds 1–4**: The ledger is visible, resting on the platform edge. A creature can see its pages and the spiral binding. No action penalty.
- **Round 5**: The ledger is half-submerged. The pages are wet and brittle. To recover the ledger and pull it to safety, a creature must reach it (30 feet of movement across the bridge and water) and succeed on a **DC 15 Strength (Athletics)** check. Failure means the creature must immediately repeat the check or fall back into the water (which is 20 feet deep).
- **Rounds 6–9**: The ledger sinks further, pages coming apart. The check to recover it increases to **DC 17 Strength (Athletics)**. Many pages are already lost.
- **Round 10**: The ledger touches the lake bottom. The remaining pages dissolve into sediment. The "read the names aloud" resolution path closes. No check can recover it now.

**Hazards**:
- The black water is 20 feet deep and bitterly cold. A creature that falls in or enters it takes 1d6 cold damage immediately and must succeed on a **DC 14 Constitution (Endurance)** check or have disadvantage on attack rolls until the end of its next turn.
- The stone bridge is only three feet wide and slippery. A creature moving across it at more than half speed must succeed on a **DC 12 Dexterity (Acrobatics)** check or slide 5 feet in a direction of your choice and fall prone.
- **Drowning**: A creature that is unconscious in the water begins drowning. It can hold its breath for 1 minute before it must start making **DC 10 Constitution** checks, beginning with one failure. On the third failure, it dies.

**Multiple Paths to Engage**:

1. **Combat Approach**: Kill or disable the two initiates before round 10. They will fight to protect the ledger and will attempt to flee if bloodied (below 11 HP). If they flee, they move toward deeper water and attempt to trigger an alarm (a low sonorous note that brings reinforcements from the Choir Gallery in 1d4+1 rounds).

2. **Ledger Recovery Approach**: Bypass or ignore the initiates entirely and attempt to cross the bridge and recover the ledger before it dissolves. A party that succeeds on the **DC 15 Strength (Athletics)** check (or higher, depending on round) can retrieve the ledger and retreat with it—preserving the "read the names aloud" resolution. The initiates will pursue, but the primary objective is secured.

3. **Ritual Disruption Approach**: A party that understands the sinking curse (via investigation, divination, or NPC information) can attempt to interrupt the initiates' coordination. If a creature uses an action to make a **DC 16 Charisma (Deception or Insight)** check, it can convince one initiate to stop pressing the ledger for 1 round. This buys time to escape or fight.

4. **Environmental Manipulation Approach**: Clever parties might redirect water flow, collapse part of the bridge, or seal the platform to prevent further sinking. These require success on **DC 13 Intelligence (Arcana or Investigation)** checks and may take more than one round to set up, but they bypass the need to fight or recover the ledger directly.

**Pressure and Tone**: The moment the party enters the Vault, narrate the weight of the space—the cold, the singing of the initiates, the sound of water dripping. On each round, note the ledger's descent: "Round 4: the water climbs to the binding... Round 5: the first page touches the surface... Round 8: the pages are almost gone." This is not a puzzle to solve once; it is a living timer that forces decision-making under pressure.

If the party dawdles or splits focus, the initiates will not wait. They will press harder, and the sinking will accelerate. The ledger is not a passive prop—its deterioration is a clock, and clocks count down whether the party is ready or not.
