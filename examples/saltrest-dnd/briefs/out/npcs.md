## Cast & Factions

## Mayor Halrik (Missing)

You know him as the aging anchor of Saltrest—weathered hands, salt-grey beard, the voice that settles disputes at the Salted Anchor. Three weeks ago, the nets began coming up empty or threaded with bone. Last Thursday night, you watched him row out alone toward the north caves, his boat's lantern cutting the dark water. He hasn't returned.

**What actually happened:** Halrik rowed to the caves seeking the cause of the strange catch. There he encountered Coria Sleet at work—not as a threat, but as something far stranger. He let her bind him with rope and lead him deeper into the Vault, where he now kneels beside the Seraphine ledger with the other Drowned Choir initiates, his hands pale and wet, pressing the ship's manifest into the black water. He is not dead, but he is no longer himself. He still knows Saltrest; he still remembers his daughter. This makes him dangerous to reach and impossible to save without destroying what he has become.

**What the party may discover:** Witnesses saw a second figure with Halrik that night—cloaked, moving with unnatural grace. A rope-seller remembers Halrik bought lanterns weeks before, not days. The drowned themselves know the mayor by name and feel no malice toward him. They speak of him with something like gratitude.

## Mother Vell (The Cleric)

The priest of a drowned-saint faith—a goddess of sailors lost at sea, whose mercy comes in the form of a swift death and a song sung over still water. Mother Vell is iron-thin, eyes storm-grey, and her fingers are stained with candlewax. She stands at the temple door each morning and refuses to bless the fishing boats anymore.

**What she fears:** Three weeks ago, Mother Vell performed the funeral rites for a drowned sailor pulled from the nets—a man named Kellan, whom she'd known since childhood. The next night, Kellan walked up the beach with seaweed in his hair and salt pooling in his mouth, his eyes open and empty. He called her name once, then turned back to the water. Since then, she has refused to bless the water, refused to touch fish still wet from the nets, and flinches from the sound of rowing.

**What she knows:** Something is wrong with death itself. The dead are not resting. She suspects Coria Sleet and magic, but more than that—she suspects her own god has abandoned Saltrest, or worse, that something older than her faith is calling the dead home. She has not left the temple in two weeks. She will not cross the breakwater.

**Why the party should listen:** Mother Vell cannot fight, but she knows the old drowned-saint rites—prayers that ease restless spirits, rituals to lay the dead to rest properly. She is a potential ally, if the party can convince her that her god still has work for her to do. Her faith is a resource; her refusal to act is a consequence.

## Coria Sleet (The Necromancer)

She is not what the village whispers. Coria is tall and precise, her robes waterlogged and clinging, her lips faintly blue with cold. She speaks in a low, certain voice—never raised, never rushed. When she smiles, it is the smile of someone doing difficult work that no one else will do.

**Her true motive:** Coria Sleet is reading the Seraphine ledger aloud to the Drowned Choir. The ship that carried them—the Seraphine—went down seventeen years ago with 143 souls. Their names were never spoken again. No prayer was sung. No one called them home.

Coria believes that raising the drowned and speaking their names aloud will *carry the souls home*—a mercy, not a curse. She sees herself as a conductor of a necessary rite, a priest of the forgotten dead. She does not hate Saltrest; she barely notices it. The village is incidental. What matters is that 143 souls, written in a waterlogged ledger in the Vault, will finally be named in the world of the living.

**What she is doing now:** Coria has bound the strongest of the drowned—including Mayor Halrik—to the Seraphine ledger in the Vault's flooded chamber. Each round they work without interruption, the ledger sinks deeper into the black water. In five rounds, it will be fully submerged (recovery requires a `DC 15 Strength (Athletics)` check). In ten rounds, the pages will dissolve entirely. Once the ledger is gone, the names can never be read aloud, and Coria's mercy becomes an eternal entombment.

**What the party may discover:** Coria was not always a necromancer. She was a sailor's widow—the Seraphine was her wife's ship. She watched the list of the dead posted on the dock, then never mentioned again. She learned magic to reach them. Every corpse she raised, every name she gathered, was an act of grief transformed into purpose. She will not surrender the ledger willingly. But she can be negotiated with, if someone understands what she is actually trying to do.

**What she knows:** The town thinks her a villain. She is indifferent to this. She knows that disruption in Saltrest will draw attention—adventurers, soldiers, priests. She is racing against time, not out of fear, but because the ledger is dissolving whether she works or not. The sea is taking it back. She is not destroying a cure; she is preserving a funeral rite.

## The Drowned Choir (The Antagonist Faction)

They are the crew of the Seraphine, seventeen years drowned, now raised by Coria and bound to a common purpose. Their bodies are waterlogged and pale, their movements slow and deliberate. Seawater drips from their clothes and beards. Some still wear the tatters of sailor's clothes. Some are missing fingers, pieces of faces. Where their eyes should show the white, there is only grey-green water.

**What they are:** They are not mindless undead. They remember their names, their shipmates, their drowning. They remember the dark water closing over them and the silence after. They speak in voices like distant waves, overlapping and sometimes harmonious. They do not hunger for flesh. They do not kill for malice.

**What they want:** They want their names spoken in the world of the living. They want to be remembered. They want the Seraphine ledger—the ship's manifest that lists them as *souls carried*—to be read aloud from beginning to end. Once this is done, Coria has promised, they will return to the deep and rest.

**How they move:** Coria has bound a core group of initiates to the Seraphine ledger itself—these are the strongest of the drowned, including Mayor Halrik. They kneel in the Vault with the ledger, pressing it deeper into the black water with a sinking curse. The rest of the Choir inhabit the three levels of Kelpwater Cove, guarding against interruption. They do not attack unless attacked; they guard. Some may warn intruders. Some may step aside, if approached with respect.

**Why they are not simply villains:** The Drowned Choir are the stakes of this adventure. The party can fight them and destroy them. They can negotiate with them and convince them to stand down. They can race to read the ledger and grant them peace. But they cannot ignore them, and they cannot save everyone. The souls of the Drowned Choir are the real terrain of this adventure—more real than any dungeon room.

**Concrete detail—the haunting:** On quiet nights in Saltrest, you can hear them singing. It sounds like the wind in the caves, like the sea working its way into wooden hulls, like voices underwater calling the names of people long dead. The song has no words, but it has a rhythm—a dirge, a roll call. Each night, it grows a little louder.
